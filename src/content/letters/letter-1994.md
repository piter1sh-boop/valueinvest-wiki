# 1994 Shareholder Letter

**Date:** March 7, 1995
**Author:** Warren Buffett
**Company:** Berkshire Hathaway

## Overview

1994 net worth increased $1.45 billion or **13.9%** (S&P 1.3%), with per-share book value reaching $10,083 — a 23% compound annual return over 30 years.

## Key Points

### The Fat Wallet Problem

> "A fat wallet is the enemy of superior investment results. And Berkshire now has a net worth of $11.9 billion compared to about $22 million when Charlie and I began."

Buffett introduced a $100 million minimum investment threshold: "We now consider a security for purchase only if we believe we can deploy at least $100 million in it."

### Ted Williams Analogy

> "My argument is, to be a good hitter, you've got to get a good ball to hit. It's the first rule in the book. If I have to bite at stuff that is out of my happy zone, I'm not a .344 hitter. I might only be a .250 hitter."

Buffett will wait for opportunities "within our own happy zone."

### Ignore Political and Economic Forecasts

> "We will continue to ignore political and economic forecasts, which are an expensive distraction for many investors."

Thirty years of unpredictable events (Vietnam War, oil shocks, Soviet Union dissolution, 508-point Dow drop) never dented Ben Graham's investment principles.

### Fear vs. Fundamentals

> "Fear is the foe of the faddist, but the friend of the fundamentalist."

Best purchases often come during "apprehensions about some macro event were at a peak."

### Coke, Gillette, Wells Fargo Stats

- Coke: 7.8% ownership → economic interest in 21 billion servings → ~$200 million earnings
- Gillette: 7% of world razor market → ~$250 million sales attributed to Berkshire
- Wells Fargo: 13% of $53 billion bank → equivalent to $7 billion "Berkshire Bank" earning ~$100 million

## Famous Quotes

> "We will not ask you to invest with us and then put our own money elsewhere."
